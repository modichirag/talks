How do we do galaxy clustering analysis?

Traditional Analysis
$\rightarrow$ Introduction
Data: a catalog of galaxies with their positions.


  1. Compute summary statistics of choice
      $\rightarrow$ generally 2-point correlation function or power spectrum
    2. $$\xi_2(|x_1-x_2|) = \langle \delta(x_1) \delta(x_2) \rangle$$

  2. Develop theoretical predictions for these statistics
      $\rightarrow$ generally based on effective field theory (EFT)

  3. Construct an analytic likelihood model
      $\rightarrow$ generally assumed to be Gaussian

  4. Inference: run MCMC to infer posterior on model parameters, $\,p(\theta | x ) \propto p(x | \theta) \ p(\theta)$

    Simulation-based inference: theory

    Traditional Analysis
    Simulation-based Inference
    $\rightarrow$ Introduction

    Generating simulations is sampling from the joint distribution, i.e., ${D} = \{x_i, \theta_i\} \sim p(x, \theta)$

    Q. How to use this for inference?
    Density estimation problem!

    Learn the likelihood distribution.
    Model the likelihood with a parametric distribution $q$$_\phi$, e.g., $\, {N}(\mu = f$$_\phi$$(\theta), \sigma = g$$_\phi$$(\theta))$

    Fit $\phi$ to
    1. (NLE) Learn the likelihood $q_{\phi^*}\rightarrow p(x|\theta)$ $$ \phi^* = \max_{\phi} \sum_i \log q_{\phi}(x_i | \theta_i) \quad \quad \{x_i, \theta_i\} \sim p(x, \theta)$$
    Or
    2. (NPE) Learn the posterior $\,q_{\phi^*}\rightarrow p(\theta|x) :$ $$ \phi^* = \max_{\phi} \sum_i \log q_{\phi}(\theta_i | x_i) \quad \quad \{x_i, \theta_i\} \sim p(x, \theta)$$

    Typically, NPE preferred over NLE
    Sufficiently flexible $q_\phi$ $\rightarrow$ parameterized as neural networks like normalizing flows
    Cranmer et al. (2020)
    Papamakarios et al. (2017)

    Simulation-based inference: in practice

    Traditional Analysis
    Simulation-based Inference
    $\rightarrow$ Introduction


    1. Training data
    Generate a dataset of simulations $$ D = \{x_i, \theta_i\}_{i\in N} \sim p(x, \theta)$$

    2. Learn the posterior
    Choose a parametric distribution $q_\phi(\theta | x)$
    Optimize to fit $\phi$ $$\phi^* = \max_{\phi} \sum_i \log q_{\phi}(\theta_i | x_i) $$
    With large number of samples $$ q_{\phi^*}(\theta | x) \approx p(\theta | x) $$
    3. Inference
    Given observed data: $x_{\rm obs}$ $$\theta \sim q_{\phi^*}(\theta | x_{\rm obs})$$

    Challenges to SBI

    High fidelity simulations: SBI training converges in the limit of a large number of samples.
    We do not have the computational resources to generate training simulations.


    • Quijote: largest simulation suite for SBI: 60 million CPU hours
      $\quad \rightarrow$ unable to capture BOSS (2015) survey volume
      $\quad \rightarrow$ Current surveys probe larger volume, at higher resolution

    • ML based emulators, e.g., CHARM
      $\quad \rightarrow$ Increasingly challenging
      $\quad \rightarrow$ Need simulations to train
      $\quad \rightarrow$ Biases due to model misspecification

    Hybrid SBI: scaling SBI to cosmological surveys

    Traditional Analysis
    Simulation-based Inference
    Challenges to SBI
    Hybrid SBI

    SBI is needed to push to smaller scales, with higher order summary statistics.
    On the largest scale, SBI is not necessary.

    Combine EFT with SBI (Modi and Philcox 2023)
    Split the data vector into two components: $\,{x} = \{{x_L}, {x_S}\}$ $$ p(x | \theta) = p(x_L | \theta) \times p(x_S |x_L, \theta) $$
    • $ p(x_L | \theta)$
      $\rightarrow$ modeled with EFT
      $\rightarrow$ $ x_L$ : any $N$-point statistic

    • $p(x_S |x_L, \theta)$
      $\rightarrow$ modeled with SBI
      $\rightarrow$ using customized small volume simulations
      $\rightarrow$ $ x_S$ : any higher order statistic, e.g., wavelets


    Leads to robust analysis by anchoring with known physics.


    Considerations for Hybrid SBI

    Traditional Analysis
    Simulation-based Inference
    Challenges to SBI
    Hybrid SBI

    1. Need to learn $p(x_S | \theta, x_L)$ instead of $p(x_S | \theta)$.
      • Cheap large volume simulation for $x_L$.
      • Simulate sub-volume for $x_S$ with high fidelity.
      • E.g., sCOLA, zoom-in simulations in galaxy formation.

    2. Modeling survey systematics using small volume simulations.
        $\rightarrow$ Monte-carlo simulations to model effects.


    3. Potential loss of constraining power:
      • Increased variance from sub-boxes.
      • Increased parameter space from combining two models.

    Summary: Simulation-based inference in cosmology


    Traditional Analysis with theoretical models
    • can only model a few summary statistics that are no longer sufficient
    • breaks down on small, non-linear scales
    • assumes a Gaussian likelihood for the data.

    Simulation based inference
    Overcomes these limitations by using simulations to model any statistic and learn its likelihood.
    • First application on data: BOSS survey with SimBIG collaboration

    Challenges to SBI
    1. Computational resources: Hybrid SBI combine EFT on large scales with SBI on small scales.
      • Learning with customized small volume simulations.
      • Variance due to using sub-boxes is sub-dominant.
      • Learn joint prior distribution to counter parameter explosion.

    2. Model-misspecification:
      • Sensitivity analyses to quantify impact of different components.
      • Generative models for better galaxy models like semi-analytic models.
