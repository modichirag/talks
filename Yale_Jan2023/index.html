Traditional cosmological analysis

a simple pipeline for galaxy clustering analysis

  • Measure the positions and estimate associated weights for each galaxy
    Weights: incompleteness, fibre-collision, FKP...

  • Compute summary statistics based on N-pt functions, e.g. power spectrum, bispectrum, 2-pt correlation function

  • Construct a theory model for the summary statistics, generally based on perturbation theory

  • Run MCMC to recover a posterior on the model parameters using an analytic likelihood
    $p(\theta | x ) \propto \underbrace{p(x | \theta)}_{\mathrm{likelihood}} \ \underbrace{p(\theta)}_{\mathrm{prior}}$

why forward modeling?


Key Idea
Instead of trying to analytical evaluate the likelihood of data summary statistics, build a computational forward model of the observables, i.e. the simulation becomes the physical model.

Benefits of computational forward modeling

  • More accurate than theory models and can be used for any summary statistic that can be evaluated computationally

  • Easy to incorporate survey systematic effects

  • Easy to combine multiple cosmological probes by joint simulations
Two flavors of forward modeling approaches

  1. Field level inference/Bayesian Heirarchical modeling

  2. Simulations based inference/Likelihood Free Inference

Field Level Inference

Field Level Inference


Key Idea
  • use simulations to model the field-level data without any compression
  • simultaneously infer the initial conditions of the Universe and the cosmological parameters.

Example: reconstruct initial conditions ($z$) by maximizing the posterior, given observed data : $$\arg\max_z \ \log p(x_{dm} | f(z)) \ + \ p(z) $$


True initial conditions
$z_0$

Reconstructed initial conditions $z$

Reconstructed dark matter distribution $x_{dm} = f(z)$

Data
$x_{dm} = f(z_0)$

Why field level inference #1


It is the optimal approach to cosmological analysis


  • No information is lost by compressing data into summary statistics

  • Undo non-linear evolution to recover the Gaussian initial conditions
    $\rightarrow$ standard reconstruction for Baryon Acoustic Oscillations
    $\rightarrow$ Power spectrum is exhaustive for Gaussian fields



Seo et al. 2007
Modi et al. 2018

Why field level inference #2


Reconstruct latent fields to enable supplementary analysis

Example: Foreground wedge in 21-cm surveys

  • Large scale modes in the wedge are lost, 20-80% of the data

  • Match field level data on small scales to recover intial conditions

  • Evolve initial conditions to reconstruct large scales

  • Enable cross-correlation analysis with photometric surveys and CMB lensing
    $\rightarrow$ most promising probes for high redshift



Other latent fields: velocity field, dark matter field, reconstruct other tracer fields

Modi et al. 2019b

Why field level inference #3


Enables joint analysis of different cosmological probes and surveys

Bottleneck: Need to model covariance matrix of different datasets

Example: reconstruct initial conditions ($s$) by maximizing the posterior, given observed data : $$\log p(z, \theta | x_1, x_2) \propto \log p(x_{1} | f(z, \theta)) + \log p(x_{2} | f(z, \theta)) \ + \ p(z) + p(\theta) $$

$$ \mathcal{L}(s) = \underbrace{[d - \mathcal{F}(s)]^\dagger N ^{-1} [d - \mathcal{F}(s)]}_{likelihood} + \underbrace{s^\dagger \mathcal{S}^{-1} s}_{prior} $$

  • Seamless interfacing with DL elements for hybrid forward models and inference

							  import flowpm

							  linear = tf.get_variable('linear', shape=(nc, nc, nc),
										 initializer=tf.random_normal_initializer(), trainable=True)
							  initial_conds = flowpm.lpt_init(linear, name='icstate')
							  final_state = flowpm.nbody(initial_conds, stages, nc, name='fnstate')

							  #Likelihood term of data
							  final = tf.zeros_like(linear)
							  final = flowpm.cic_paint(final, final_state[0], boxsize=bs, name='final')
							  residual = tf.multiply(tf.subtract(final, data), 1/sigma)
							  likelihood = tf.reduce_sum(tf.multiply(residual, residual))
							  #likelihood = module(dict(features=final, labels=data), as_dict=True)[loss] ##DL
							  #Prior
							  lineark = flowpm.r2c3d(linear, norm=nc**3)
							  prior = tf.square(tf.cast(tf.abs(lineark), tf.float32))
							  prior = tf.reduce_sum(tf.multiply(prior, 1/priorwt))j
							  #Loss
							  loss = tf.add(likelihood, prior, name='loss')
							  optimizer = ScipyOptimizerInterface(loss, var_list=[linear], 'L-BFGS-B')

							  with tf.Session() as session:
							      optimizer.minimize(session)

Lagrangian bias model

  • Weight the particles with lagrangian (initial) field
    • $$w = 1 + \mathbf{b_1} \delta_L(q)+ \mathbf{b_2} (\delta^2(q) - \langle \delta^2_L(q)\rangle) + \mathbf{b_g} (g_L(q) - \langle g_L(q)\rangle) $$

  • Displace them to the Eulerian (redshift space) position with N-body dynamics
    • $$ x= q + \Psi$$ $$\delta^b_{\rm HI} (x) = \delta_{[1]}(x) + \mathbf{b_1}\delta_{[\delta_L]}(x) + \mathbf{b_2}\delta_{[\delta^2_L]}(x) + \mathbf{b_g}\delta_{[g_L]}(x)$$

  • Fit bias parameters to minimize the difference at the field level
    $$ P_{\rm err} (k) = \frac{1}{N_{\rm modes}(k)} \sum_{\vec{k}, |\vec{k}| \sim k} | \delta^b_{\rm HI}(\vec{k}) - \delta_{\rm HI}(\vec{k})| ^2 $$

Schmittfull 2018, Modi et al. 2019b,c
